Television Star Vincent Whittman: The Largest Media Cover-Up in American Television History
For nearly two decades he was one of the most beloved figures in American broadcasting. But newly uncovered evidence suggests the truth behind Vincent Whittman was far darker than audiences ever imagined.
By Margaret Ellison
Published May 14, 1979
A production camera from the early era of live television, when Vincent Whittman's program dominated evening broadcasts.

For much of the 1950s, Vincent Whittman was the face of American television. His nightly variety program, The Vincent Whittman Show, was watched by millions across the country and made him one of the most recognizable entertainers of the early broadcasting era.

To audiences, he appeared charming, quick-witted and effortlessly confident — a man who could guide interviews, introduce musical guests and deliver biting jokes without missing a beat. Industry executives praised his ability to command a live audience, while viewers regarded him as a comforting presence in their living rooms.

But according to investigators and historians now reexamining the era, the persona presented to the public may have concealed something far more sinister.

Court records released this year describe a pattern of disappearances and suspicious deaths within Whittman's professional circle spanning nearly two decades. Several of those incidents, once considered unrelated accidents, are now believed to have been connected to the host himself.

The case has stunned historians of American media, not only because of the scale of the allegations, but because of how thoroughly the entertainment industry appeared to ignore or suppress warning signs at the time.

“It raises disturbing questions about how much was known,” said cultural historian Daniel Mercer. “Whittman was one of the most powerful personalities in television. Challenging him was simply not something people were willing to do.”

Perhaps the most infamous moment occurred during a live broadcast in 1953, when a stunt involving a rotating target wheel went catastrophically wrong, resulting in the death of a stage assistant. At the time, the incident was publicly described as a tragic accident.

Decades later, investigators would revisit the recording and conclude the circumstances surrounding the event were far from accidental.

Whittman died in 1958 while serving a prison sentence stemming from the televised incident. Yet the full scope of his alleged crimes only began to emerge years later, prompting what some legal scholars have described as one of the most extraordinary posthumous criminal cases in American history.
